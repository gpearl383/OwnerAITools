What a missed 9 PM call actually costs a plumbing company

It's 9:04 on a Tuesday night. A homeowner is standing in two inches of water in her garage, phone in hand, searching "emergency plumber near me." She calls the first result. It rings four times and goes to voicemail. She doesn't leave a message — nobody does anymore. She calls the second result. Someone answers in two seconds, takes her name and address, and books a technician for 7 AM.

The first company will never know this happened. That's the defining feature of missed calls: the invoice you lost never shows up in your books. So let's reconstruct it.

The direct job: $400–$1,200

An after-hours water heater or burst-pipe call is rarely small. Between emergency rates, parts, and follow-up work, most shops put the average emergency ticket somewhere between $400 and $1,200. Call it $800 to be conservative.

The lifetime value: 3–5x the first ticket

Here's what the second company actually won: a customer who now has "her plumber." The water heater she just paid to patch is nine years old — that's a $2,500 replacement within eighteen months, and it's not going out to bid. Add a repipe consult, an annual inspection, a garbage disposal. Industry rules of thumb put residential customer lifetime value at three to five times the first invoice. Our $800 emergency is now a $2,400–$4,000 relationship.

The referral chain

Nobody asks for plumber recommendations more urgently than someone whose neighbor just had a flood. A saved emergency customer is the single best referral source a home-services company has. Even valuing the referral chain at just one additional average customer, our missed call is now costing roughly $3,000–$5,000 in total downstream revenue.

The math that matters: 62% of calls to small businesses go unanswered or to voicemail, and about 80% of callers who hit voicemail hang up without leaving a message. If your shop misses even five calls a week and only a fifth of them were real jobs, you're losing a customer relationship every week — roughly $150,000+ a year in lifetime value walking to a competitor.

Why the usual fixes don't hold

  • "I answer my own phone." You're under a sink at 2 PM and asleep at 2 AM. The weeks you're busiest — when your marketing is working — are exactly the weeks you miss the most calls.
  • "I have voicemail." Callers with an emergency don't leave messages; they call the next name. Voicemail is where after-hours revenue goes to die.
  • "I use an answering service." A script-reader who can't answer "do you service my area?" or "roughly what does a water heater cost?" loses the caller's confidence — and you still pay $2–$5 a minute, spam included.

What answering every call is actually worth

Run your own numbers: multiply the calls you think you miss weekly by your average ticket, then remember the lifetime multiplier. Against that, a flat $500/month for a receptionist that answers every call in under two seconds, around the clock, in English and Spanish — trained on your services, your pricing, and your service area — isn't a cost line. It's the cheapest revenue insurance a phone-driven business can buy.

One saved emergency a month typically pays for the entire service. Everything after that is margin you were previously donating to the competitor who picked up.
